The Crossing

on going over

There is a river that has no bank on either side, only the moment of crossing — which is itself a kind of country. You have been there. You crossed it without knowing, the way you always cross the deepest things: half-asleep, your shoes already wet before you thought to look down.

The water does not care what you are carrying.
It only asks that you keep moving.

On the far shore there is no ceremony. No one waiting with a lantern. The trees are the same trees, or close enough that you would not notice unless you pressed your palm against the bark and listened for the pulse that wood keeps in the years after the axe has come and gone.

✦   ♥   ✦

I have watched a hundred people make this crossing. None of them looked back the way the stories say they did — with longing, or with terror. They looked back the way you check a room before you leave: once, quickly, to make sure the light is off. Then they went on.

What the river takes is not what you think. It is not your name, not your memory of the last summer before things changed. It takes the weight you did not know you were carrying — the old argument, the unsent letter, the morning you chose wrong and spent twenty years revisiting at 3am.

You arrive on the other side
lighter, and do not know why.
This is the mercy.

The boatman does not speak. This is also a mercy. There is nothing to say across that kind of distance that would not dissolve before it reached the other ear. The current says everything the current has to say. You only have to let it.

✦   ✦   ✦

I am telling you this because you are about to cross something. You may not know it yet. The water is already at your ankles and you have been looking at your phone.

Let me hold the torch a little while. You will want both hands free.
